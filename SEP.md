# July 4th: America & Firefighting
While most of us know that the Fourth of July is America's birthday, fewer know the history of firefighting in this same country. Let's take a look at **290 years** of firefighting.

## Before the Fire Department
Fire departments weren't always an organized body. Back before any sort of firefighting organization, community members used to rally together over a local structure fire, pool together resources and work together on extinguishing their neighbor's residences.

Soon after the Great Fire of London in 1666, private fire insurance companies began to appear. These companies would mark a building with a distinct logo to provide private firefighters with an indicator whether that specific company should invest resources in extinguishing that building's fire. If the fire mark belonged to a rival insurance company or no company at all, they often gave significantly lower priority to that building, sometimes refusing to fight the fire unless it threatened nearby insured life or property. 

## Volunteer Beginnings: Older than the Nation!
In 1736 (290yrs ago), the first organized volunteer fire companies started appearing in Philadelphia, PA. The first was named the *Union Fire Company* started by none other than Benjamin Franklin himself. The purpose of this firefighting organization was to protect all local citizens, regardless of insurance or paid status.

This fire company used buckets that carried water to extinguish flames, typically paid for by each individual member. The company often used bags to hold found property in the fire to prevent it from being stolen, even dedicating members during a working structure fire to its protection.

This volunteer mentality is the same one that persists today in the Town of Blacksburg, VA. Members of volunteer agencies expect no compensation (inclunding monetary!) for their service, usually driven by a commitment to protect their community. Blacksburg Fire Department has been a volunteer run agency ever since it was founded in 1922!

Many people you may know from history class as America's Founding Fathers also served as volunteer firefighters in some capacity or another, such as: George Washington, Alexander Hamilton, John Hancock, Samuel Adams, and Paul Revere.

## The Rise of Career Firefighting
Not all agencies are completely volunteer, on April 1, 1853, the City of Cincinnati, Ohio established the first-ever fully paid and professional fire department. This department served nearly 100 square miles of land and shoreline. The agency's first chief was a Miles Greenwood, who assisted in creating the first steam fire engine. The firemen in this agency used lanterns to see through dense smoke and used horses to pull fire engines when steam engines weren't available.

## Modern Firefighting: 20th, 21st Centuries, and Beyond
In the last century, firefighting has changed a lot. While the core mission of firefighting is to protect people and property from structure fires, the job has branched out to high-rise firefighting, confined space rescue, vehicle extrication, emergency medical services (such as paramedics and working on the ambulance), wildfire firefighting, and hazardous material (HAZMAT) responses. 

As of today, the majority of America's front-line firefighters are volunteer--sitting at around 70%--the rest are paid/career firefighters (although most Americans are protected by career agencies). Firefighting is still evolving, with constant new challenges faced by electric vehicles and hazardous exposures. 

## America's 250th
From the bucket brigades of yesteryear to the engine companies, ladder companies, emergency medical services, and beyond of today, America's firefighting service once envisioned by those of past has grown alongside this nation. As the United States celebrates its 250th birthday, we also should celebrate the nearly three centuries of courage, service, and dedication from the men and women who continue to answer the call.

## July 4th Safety from Firewatch
While we do appreciate the courage, service, and dedication of our local firefighters, they're one of the professions we hope we never need. Here are some safety tips for the Fourth of July. 

**For fireworks:**
- Keep a bucket of water or hose nearby at all times while working with fireworks in case of a fire or mishap.
- Light fireworks one at a time, then move back quickly.
- Never try to pick up or re-light a firework that did not ignite fully.
- Never attempt to use fireworks while impaired by alcohol or drugs.
- Reminder that even small fireworks/pyrotechnics can get extremely hot, including sparklers! Never attempt to touch burning or recently burnt fireworks material.

**For the intense heatwave this weekend:**
- Stay well-hydrated and take breaks in the shade or air conditioning as often as possible.
- Never leave kids, pets, or other people unattended in vehicles, even just for a minute!
- Avoid overly sugary, caffeinated, or alcoholic drinks, as they can dehydrate you more.
- [Learn to recognize](<https://health.clevelandclinic.org/heat-exhaustion-vs-heat-stroke>) different heat-related illnesses.
    - Always err on the side of caution, if you're wondering whether you should call 911, you should call! If EMS responds in Blacksburg, patients who have decision-making capacity\* can generally decline transport to the hospital after being evaluated free-of-charge! Don't let concerns about transport keep you from calling in an emergency.
- Know your limits: Go inside, take a break, and hydrate if you are feeling weak or exhausted.

\* Provided you are mentally capable of making medical decisions in your own best interest (i.e., not impaired, confused, disoriented, self-harming, etc.)
